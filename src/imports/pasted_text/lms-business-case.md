1.0           Business Case



1.0 Introduction and Business Background

 

The university’s core mission is to deliver high-quality, accessible education and to maintain its competitiveness among peer institutions. The Chief Technology Officer (CTO) believes the university can streamline academic operations and strengthen the student experience by implementing a centralized Learning Management System (LMS) that consolidates course delivery, assessment, and communication into a single platform accessible to lecturers, students, and academic administrators.

 

2.0 Business Objective

 

The university’s strategic goals include improving student satisfaction, operational efficiency, and competitiveness in student recruitment and retention. The LMS project will support these goals by giving lecturers and students a single platform for course materials, assignments, and grades, thereby reducing the administrative burden of manual record-keeping and improving the consistency of course delivery across faculties. Because the university evaluates new initiatives based on measurable efficiency gains and student outcomes, this project must demonstrate a clear, quantifiable return before proceeding.

 

3.0 Current Situation and Problem/Opportunity Statement

 

The university currently relies on a combination of e-mail, printed handouts, and informal messaging tools to distribute course materials and communicate with students. Lecturers manually record attendance, assignment submissions, and grades using spreadsheets. The university also maintains a student information system (SIS) that tracks enrolment and timetable data, but it is not linked to any platform used for day-to-day course delivery. There is an opportunity to introduce a centralized LMS that consolidates course content, assessment, and communication, reducing administrative workload while giving students and lecturers consistent, anytime access to course information.

 

4.0 Critical Assumptions and Constraints

 

The proposed LMS must be a valuable asset to the university, and current lecturers and students must actively support its adoption. It must run primarily on the university’s existing IT infrastructure, requiring minimal additional capital expenditure and minimal ongoing technical support. The system must integrate with the existing student information system, be accessible only to authorized users, and comply with the university’s data protection policies. The CTO requires that the project pay for itself in efficiency and retention benefits within approximately one year of full deployment, and that the system be piloted and substantially operational within one academic year.

 

5.0 Analysis of Options and Recommendation

 

There are three options for addressing this opportunity:

1.     Do nothing. The university continues to operate with its current mix of e-mail, spreadsheets, and informal tools.

2.     Purchase a commercial, ready-made LMS (Software-as-a-Service) with minimal customization.

3.     Implement an open-source LMS core, customized in-house to integrate with the university’s existing systems and academic workflows.

 

Based on discussions with stakeholders, we believe that Option 3 is the best option, as it offers the strongest balance of cost, functionality, and long-term flexibility while avoiding dependence on a single commercial vendor.

 

6.0 Preliminary Project Requirements

 

The main features of the LMS project include the following:

1.     Course content management. Lecturers must be able to upload, organize, and publish lecture notes, slides, videos, and reading materials, and students must be able to search and access this content easily. Lecturers must also be able to submit new course templates, which should first be reviewed by the IT department.

2.     Assessment and grading. Users must be able to create, submit, and grade assignments and quizzes, with automated grading available for objective question types, reducing the time lecturers spend on routine marking.

3.     Communication tools. The system must provide announcements, discussion forums, and an “Ask the Lecturer” feature to help students get timely answers and build stronger engagement with course staff.

4.     Integration with the student information system (SIS). The LMS must automatically synchronize enrolment and timetable data to avoid duplicate data entry and reduce administrative errors.

5.     Security and access control. The system must restrict access by role (student, lecturer, administrator) and comply with the university’s data protection policies.

6.     Reporting dashboards. The system must provide real-time visibility of student academic progress to lecturers, academic advisors, and the registry.

7.     Other features suggested by users, if they add value to the university and its students.

 

7.0 Budget Estimate and Financial Analysis

 

A preliminary estimate of the initial project cost is RM150,000. This estimate is based on the project manager working approximately 600 hours at RM60 per hour, technical project team members working a combined 1,500 hours at RM70 per hour, and RM9,000 for outsourced LMS software, plugins, and setup services. After the project is completed, annual maintenance costs of RM45,000 are included for each of the following three years, primarily for hosting, licensing, and ongoing technical support.

 

Projected benefits are based on a reduction in hours that academic and administrative staff spend on manual record-keeping and routine administrative tasks. If each of 200 staff members saved just 25 hours per year and that time is valued conservatively at RM40 per hour, the projected benefit from time savings would be RM200,000 per year. If the new LMS improves student retention and recruitment enough to increase the university’s net revenue by just 1 percent, using past financial data, the additional benefit would be at least RM50,000 each year. Total projected annual benefits are therefore approximately RM250,000.

 

Exhibit A summarizes the projected costs and benefits and shows the estimated net present value (NPV), return on investment (ROI), and the year in which payback occurs. It also lists the assumptions used in this preliminary financial analysis. The financial estimates are encouraging: the estimated payback occurs within Year 1, as required by the CTO. The NPV is approximately RM378,900, and the discounted ROI based on a three-year system life is approximately 142 percent.

 

8.0 Schedule Estimate

 

The CTO would like the LMS piloted and operational within six months, with full university-wide rollout completed within one academic year. There is some flexibility in the schedule to accommodate the academic calendar and examination periods. We also assume that the new system will have a useful life of at least three years, consistent with the financial analysis in Exhibit A.

 

9.0 Potential Risks

 

This project carries several risks. The foremost risk is a lack of adoption by lecturers and students; active use by both groups is essential to realizing the projected efficiency and retention benefits. There are some technical risks associated with integrating the LMS with the existing student information system and ensuring adequate data security, but the core features of the system rely on proven, widely adopted technologies. The main business risk is investing the time and money required for this project without realizing the projected benefits, for example if user adoption remains low after rollout.
